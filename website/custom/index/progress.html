The progress humanity made in the last 200 years

An easy-to-follow infographic showing how far we've come.

Child Mortality

The percentage of children that died in the first five years of their lives:

In 1800 43% of children died in before the age of five, in 2015 it was only 4%.

Life Expectancy

The life expectancy of the average person in the United Kingdom:

In 1803 an average person in the United Kingdom could expect to live 40 years, in 2015 an average person can expect to live more than twice as long - 81.5 years.

Extreme Poverty

The percentage of people in the world that are living in extreme poverty:

In 1820 almost 95% of people lived in extreme poverty, in 2015 less than 10% of people were living in conditions of extreme poverty.

World's GDP

The following graph shows GDP per capita (in 2011 US Dollars) of the world which is a metric of the combined value of all goods and services produced per person.

In 1870 on average a person produced goods and services worth $1,263 whereas in 2016 an average person produced $14,574 in goods and services. That's over ten times as much!

Literacy

Out of every hundred people, on average how many can read:

In 1800 only 12 out of 100 people could read, whereas in 2014 it was over 85 out of 100 people. Nearly 4 times as many.

Gender Equality

The pay gap between women and men in the UK, as a percentage of male earnings:

In 1970 median income for men was nearly 50% higher compared to women. By 2015, that had decreased 17%.

But we still face many challenges...

17,000 children die each day because of conditions of poverty.

Source: Unicef

Over 1,000 people die each day because of malaria.

Source: World Health Organisation

844 million people lack basic access to drinking water.

Source: World Health Organisation

Experts estimate that there is an almost 1 in 5 chance of humanity's extinction before 2100 due to global catastrophic risks

Source: Oxford University Future of Humanity Institute

Roughly 11 billion animals are raised and slaughtered under extreme suffering in factory farms each year in the US alone

Source: Humane Society

...and YOU have the power to help

It has never been more possible for you to have a meaningful, positive impact on a massive scale. Using scientific methods we can now figure out which interventions bring the most good, prevent the most suffering, and save the most lives.

For example:

  • treating one child from schistosomiasis, the most deadly neglected tropical disease, costs about $1
  • protecting two people from malaria by distributing bed nets costs approximately $5
  • providing food-based micronutrient fortification for 1 person for 1 year costs only 53 cents
  • protecting a child from worm infection costs only 50 cents per year
